## 2. Why Now

**2.1 Climate disruption is quantified, not projected**

Global wine production in 2024 fell to its lowest level since 1961, estimated at 225–235 million hectolitres — approximately 13% below the 10-year average [Source: OIV First Estimates, November 2024]. France saw a 23.5% decline from 2023 to its lowest harvest since 1957. These figures are not forecasts — they are outcomes already registered.

The peer-reviewed science is consistent: warming conditions are advancing harvest dates, increasing drought and heat stress events, and altering disease pressure patterns. One landmark study (PNAS, Wolkovich et al.) found harvest dates advanced by approximately 6 days per degree Celsius of warming. Research published in *Nature Reviews Earth & Environment* (2024) documents compounding risks including increased pathogen pressure, fruit composition changes, and habitat suitability loss for traditional wine regions [Source: Ollat et al., Nature Reviews Earth & Environment, 2024].

The estimate that up to 90% of traditional wine regions in lowland Mediterranean areas, southern California, and coastal regions could face viability risk by end-of-century under high-emission scenarios represents peer-reviewed scientific consensus, not advocacy [Source: Hannah et al., PNAS, 2013; updated in subsequent literature].

**2.2 Regulatory pressure is real and accelerating**

The EU Farm to Fork Strategy (2020) targets a 50% reduction in use and risk of synthetic pesticides by 2030, with the most hazardous substances targeted separately at 50% reduction [Source: European Commission, Farm to Fork Strategy]. Viticulture uses approximately 70% of fungicides applied in the EU — making wine production uniquely exposed to this regulatory trajectory [Source: USDA FAS report on EU pesticide initiatives].

The EU Soil Monitoring Law entered into force on December 16, 2025, establishing mandatory soil health monitoring frameworks across Member States and naming regenerative agriculture as a core remediation pathway [Source: European Commission, Soil Monitoring Law].

The EU Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) applies mandatory sustainability disclosure requirements to approximately 50,000 companies operating in or trading with the EU. Large wine groups and their suppliers are within scope [Source: European Commission, CSRD; Akin Gump, 2024]. This creates measurable and growing demand for GHG accounting, soil health data, and supply chain traceability tools.

**2.3 Capital is rotating toward this thesis**

Global agrifoodtech investment reached approximately USD 16 billion in 2024, with climate-resilient farming, biologicals, and carbon tracking among the sustained investment priorities [Source: AgFunder Global AgriFoodTech Investment Report 2024]. Early-stage investment declined 33.7% year-over-year — a signal that quality selection discipline is increasing, not that the thesis is weakening. Investors now require near-term scalability and adoption evidence, not just technical promise.

## 3. Regenerative Viticulture Thesis

**3.1 Definition**

Regenerative viticulture is not a legally standardized term but is operationally defined across the industry as a set of vineyard management practices designed to restore soil biological activity, increase above- and below-ground biodiversity, reduce external inputs (synthetic pesticides and fertilizers), improve water retention, and enable carbon sequestration [Source: Springer Nature, *Discover Sustainability*, 2024; NRDC Regenerative Agriculture 101].

Key practices include:
- Cover cropping and reduced or no-till soil management
- Compost and biological soil amendments
- Biological pest control and integrated pest management (IPM)
- Precision irrigation for water optimization
- Monitoring and measurement of soil carbon, GHG fluxes, and biological indicators

A peer-reviewed study published in *Frontiers in Sustainable Food Systems* (2023) specifically quantified soil carbon sequestration rates from regenerative practices in vineyards, documenting measurable improvements in soil organic carbon when cover cropping, reduced tillage, and compost applications were combined [Source: Frontiers in Sustainable Food Systems, 2023].

**3.2 Why regenerative viticulture needs technology**

The transition to regenerative practices is knowledge-, monitoring-, and data-intensive. Growers cannot optimize what they cannot measure. This creates a natural technology wedge:

- **Diagnostics:** Soil microbiome analysis (Biome Makers, Trace Genomics) to understand baseline biology and guide input decisions
- **Biological inputs:** Biostimulants and biopesticides (Biorizon Biotech, Mycophyto) to replace or reduce synthetic inputs
- **Monitoring:** Real-time carbon flux sensors (Agrology), satellite-based vineyard analytics (Deep Planet), and weather/disease models (Sencrop, now ISAGRI) to quantify outcomes
- **Verification:** Carbon MRV platforms (EZ Lab Wine Blockchain, MyEasyFarm, ClimateHound, FarmLab) to generate auditable ESG and carbon data

**3.3 Regulatory and buyer tailwinds**

- EU Farm to Fork: 50% pesticide reduction by 2030 mandates agrochemical alternatives [Source: European Commission]
- EU Soil Monitoring Law (entered into force December 2025): requires systematic soil health monitoring, creating demand for soil diagnostics tools [Source: European Commission]
- CSRD: mandatory GHG and sustainability disclosures for large companies operating in the EU, cascading requirements down supply chains [Source: European Commission CSRD]
- Retailer ESG requirements: major retailers (e.g., Waitrose, Carrefour, Tesco) increasingly require supply chain sustainability data from wine producers — a commercial pull force operating independently of regulation

## 4. Research Methodology

**4.1 Company universe construction**

A total of 96 companies were identified through systematic mapping of the regenerative viticulture value chain across seven segments: (1) Precision viticulture / vineyard management, (2) Soil health and biological inputs, (3) Irrigation optimization, (4) Pest management, (5) Carbon MRV and traceability, (6) Wine production technologies, and (7) Packaging and circular economy.

Geographic scope was global, with emphasis on Europe (Mediterranean wine regions), North America (US and Canada), Australasia, and Latin America.

**4.2 Scoring model**

A five-dimension weighted scoring model was applied to the full 96-company universe:

| Dimension | Weight | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| Market Segment Attractiveness | 25% | Segments with stronger regulatory tailwinds and investable fundamentals |
| Technology & Business Model | 25% | SaaS and biotech models have superior scalability and margin characteristics |
| Company Maturity (via team size proxy) | 20% | Targeting Series A–B sweet spot: proven but scalable |
| Impact & ESG Alignment | 15% | Relevance to regenerative agriculture and carbon/biodiversity outcomes |
| Differentiation & Competitive Edge | 15% | IP, data moats, regulatory barriers, and defensible technology |

94 of 96 companies scored as "high confidence" scoreable. The model produced a ranked list of 20 companies with scores ranging from 4.85 (joint top) to 4.15.

**4.3 Adjustments applied**

One company was excluded from investable consideration despite high scoring:
- **Sencrop** — original #3 (score 4.55): acquired by Groupe ISAGRI effective January 7, 2025. No longer independently investable. Excluded from consideration.

**4.4 Deep research phase**

Deep research was conducted on the final top-3 companies (Biome Makers, Biorizon Biotech, Agrology), including review of funding records, named customer deployments, scientific partnerships, regulatory certifications, and competitive positioning. Red flags and data gaps were documented for each company.

## 6. Segment Attractiveness

### 6.1 Precision Viticulture Software

**What it includes:** AI-based vineyard monitoring, satellite and drone imagery analytics, disease prediction models, yield forecasting, and multi-parameter decision support platforms. Also includes connected weather stations and microclimate networks.

**Why it matters:** Climate adaptation in viticulture is fundamentally a data problem. Growers cannot respond to increasing variability without better information. Precision viticulture software is the enabling layer for all other regenerative practices.

**Key demand drivers:**
- Climate variability increasing monitoring requirements
- Satellite and IoT costs declining, improving unit economics
- Regulatory spray-reduction requirements creating demand for decision tools (e.g., fungicide spray timing based on disease models)

**Market size:** The global precision viticulture market was valued at USD 1.6–1.9 billion in 2024, projected to reach USD 2.3–6.1 billion by 2030–2033 at a CAGR of 12–14% [Source: Mordor Intelligence / Precedence Research / IMARC Group, 2024–2025]. Note: estimates vary materially by scope and methodology — these should be treated as directional proxies, not precise TAMs.

**Software segment note:** Software is the fastest-growing component within precision viticulture, projected at a 17.2% CAGR through 2030, as SaaS layers are added to existing sensor infrastructure [Source: Mordor Intelligence, 2025].

**Investment attractiveness:** HIGH. Scalable SaaS economics, strong tailwinds, recurring revenue. Caution: market is becoming crowded; differentiation on data quality and decision-value (not just dashboards) is key.

**Key risks:** Commoditization of satellite data; customer acquisition cost in fragmented grower market; hardware-SaaS companies carry mixed margin profiles.

### 6.2 Soil Health and Biological Inputs

**What it includes:** Soil microbiome diagnostics (DNA-based analysis), biostimulants, biopesticides, mycorrhizal inoculants, biochar, and cover crop seed mixes. Both analytical services and physical input products.

**Why it matters:** Soil health is the foundational KPI of regenerative agriculture. Measurement without inputs, and inputs without measurement, both limit regenerative transition effectiveness. This segment sits at the core of the investment thesis.

**Key demand drivers:**
- EU Farm to Fork: 50% synthetic pesticide reduction target [Source: European Commission]
- EU biostimulant regulation (Regulation EU 2019/1009) providing standardized regulatory pathway for biological inputs
- Carbon markets requiring verifiable soil carbon — healthy soils sequester more carbon
- Consumer and retailer demand for organic and regenerative certified wines

**Market size:** The global agricultural biologicals market (biostimulants + biopesticides) was valued at approximately USD 11.8–14.2 billion in 2024, with a CAGR of 9–11% projected through 2030–2034 [Source: Precedence Research / MarketsandMarkets, 2024]. This is an adjacent/proxy market — viticulture-specific biologicals are a subset. No verified viticulture-specific biologicals TAM is available from public sources; estimate the viticulture segment at approximately 5–8% of the total, implying USD 600M–1.1B, given wine represents approximately 5–7% of global agriculture by planted area.

**Investment attractiveness:** HIGH. Strong regulatory tailwinds, documented agronomic impact, growing corporate demand for verifiable biological inputs. Pure-product companies face margin pressure from large agrochemical acquirers.

**Key risks:** Regulatory registration timelines in new geographies; competition from large agrichemical companies pivoting to biologicals (BASF, Syngenta/Valagro, Bayer); production scale-up challenges for biotech manufacturing.

### 6.3 Precision Irrigation

**What it includes:** Soil moisture sensors, plant-based stress monitoring, AI-driven irrigation scheduling, drip system control, and evapotranspiration-based decision support.

**Why it matters:** Water scarcity is the most immediate climate-driven constraint on viticulture in Mediterranean and semi-arid wine regions. Water regulation is tightening in key wine regions (California, Spain, Italy, Chile). Irrigation optimization directly reduces water use and energy costs.

**Key demand drivers:**
- Water scarcity and drought in Mediterranean, Californian, and Chilean wine regions
- Water pricing regulations and agricultural water-use quotas
- Documented water savings of 15–60% from precision irrigation vs. conventional systems [Source: Emergen Research; various vineyard case studies]

**Market size:** The global precision irrigation market was valued at approximately USD 6.4–7.2 billion in 2024, projected to reach approximately USD 19 billion by 2034 at an 11.4% CAGR [Source: Emergen Research / MarketsandMarkets, 2024]. Viticulture is one of the highest-value precision irrigation verticals given crop value per hectare, but no separate viticulture-specific TAM is publicly verified.

**Investment attractiveness:** MEDIUM-HIGH. Large market, strong drought-driven demand. Hardware companies face margin compression; software-augmented platforms (WiseConn, SupPlant, Kilimo) are more attractive.

**Key risks:** Hardware manufacturing costs and deployment complexity; competitor pressure from large precision agriculture platforms (Lindsay, Trimble); price sensitivity in lower-value wine regions.

### 6.4 Carbon MRV and Traceability

**What it includes:** Agricultural carbon measurement, reporting, and verification tools; soil carbon sensors and models; supply chain traceability platforms; carbon footprint calculators; digital product identity systems.

**Why it matters:** Wineries and winery supply chains are increasingly subject to mandatory GHG disclosure (CSRD) and voluntary carbon market participation. The segment is early-stage but strategically critical — it is the infrastructure layer that gives all other regenerative practices financial and regulatory value.

**Key demand drivers:**
- EU CSRD mandatory sustainability disclosure (50,000 companies in scope) [Source: European Commission]
- Voluntary carbon markets — agricultural soil carbon credits are a growing offset category
- Retailer ESG requirements cascading sustainability disclosure down wine supply chains
- Growing consumer demand for transparent carbon labeling

**Market size (proxy):** The global voluntary carbon market for agriculture, forestry, and land use is projected at approximately USD 7.5 billion in 2025, growing at approximately 28% CAGR to 2026 [Source: GlobeNewswire / Research and Markets, January 2026]. The broader voluntary carbon market reached approximately USD 2.2 billion in 2024 [Source: Growth Market Reports]. Note: the agricultural carbon credit segment experienced contraction in 2023–2024 due to quality controversies affecting voluntary markets. These figures represent addressable opportunity, not current run-rate demand for agri-MRV software.

**Investment attractiveness:** MEDIUM-HIGH with high optionality. Near-term demand is regulatory (ESG reporting tools); medium-term demand is market-driven (carbon credits). Companies that can serve both use cases with verified, sensor-backed data are best positioned.

**Key risks:** Voluntary carbon market quality crisis (Verra credit controversies 2023–2024) creating buyer hesitancy; regulatory carbon frameworks (CBAM, Article 6) not yet mature for agriculture; carbon registry acceptance of sensor-based MRV methodologies still pending for most providers.

### 6.5 Pest and Disease Management

**What it includes:** Biological pest control (beneficial insects, pheromone mating disruption, biopesticides), AI-based disease prediction models, remote scouting tools, and precision spraying systems.

**Why it matters:** Crop protection is the largest single operating cost in European viticulture. The EU's 50% pesticide reduction mandate creates direct demand for IPM solutions. Climate change is increasing disease pressure (downy mildew, powdery mildew, Botrytis) in historically cooler wine regions.

**Key demand drivers:**
- EU Farm to Fork pesticide reduction mandate
- Consumer and certification demand for organic and low-residue wines
- Disease model-driven spray reduction (documented 10–30% reductions in cooperative programs — e.g., Sencrop/ISAGRI data) [Source: company data, internal scoring notes]

**Market size (proxy):** The broader global biopesticides market is a sub-segment of the USD 11.8–14.2 billion agricultural biologicals figure cited above [Source: Precedence Research, 2024]. No verified viticulture-specific pest management TAM is publicly available.

**Investment attractiveness:** MEDIUM. Strong regulatory demand, but competitive landscape increasingly crowded with large players. Biological pest control manufacturers (Koppert, Andermatt Biocontrol) are established; pure-play viticulture pest tech remains niche.

**Key risks:** Biological control product registrations are multi-year and jurisdiction-specific; efficacy variability vs. synthetic alternatives; buyer price sensitivity for physical biocontrol products.

### 6.7 Packaging and Circular Economy

**What it includes:** Sustainable wine packaging alternatives (lightweight glass, recycled PET, paper-based packaging), cork recycling programs, and winery waste valorization.

**Why it matters:** Approximately 46% of GHG emissions from grape-to-bottle wine production originate from glass production [Source: various industry sources citing glass vs. alternative format LCA data]. **Key demand drivers:**
- EU packaging regulations requiring recyclability and recycled content
- Corporate GHG scope 3 reduction commitments driving bottle weight reduction
- Consumer acceptance of alternative wine packaging is growing (boxed wine sales at Waitrose +18% in 2024) [Source: Wine Industry Advisor / Just Drinks, 2024]

**Market size:** The global wine packaging market was valued at USD 6.1 billion in 2023, projected to reach USD 11.8 billion by 2034 at a 6.2% CAGR [Source: Towards Packaging, 2024].

**Investment attractiveness:** MEDIUM. Large market, clear regulatory pressure. However, this segment is dominated by industrial packaging companies; pure circular economy startups face scaling and cost challenges relative to incumbent glass manufacturers.

**Key risks:** Consumer perception of alternative packaging (still a quality signal barrier in premium segments); glass industry lobbying against packaging regulation; thin margins for recycling-focused businesses.

## 7. Top 20 Screening Logic

**7.1 Scoring outcomes**

Applying the weighted five-dimension model to all 96 companies produced scores ranging from 4.85 (top) to below 2.0 for the lowest-ranked companies. The top-20 threshold sits at a score of 4.15. All 20 have "high" scoring confidence.

The model heavily rewarded:
- Companies in soil health/biologicals and carbon MRV segments (highest market attractiveness scores: 5/5)
- Pure biotech or SaaS technology models (highest tech/business model scores: 5/5)
- Companies in the 50–100 employee range (optimal maturity bracket: 5/5)

**7.2 Key divergence from reference list**

The original (reference) top-20 list, compiled via qualitative analyst review, was heavily weighted toward precision viticulture and irrigation names (Arable #1, WiseConn #3, Naïo Technologies #4). The computed top-20, applying quantitative scoring, produced a substantially different result:

- **Biome Makers** moved to #1 (was #2 in reference): highest score (4.85) driven by optimal scores on all five dimensions
- **Biorizon Biotech** entered at #2 (absent from reference): not previously included; strong biotech moat and EU regulatory validation drove high scores
- **15 companies in the computed top-20 were absent from the reference list**, largely because the reference over-weighted precision viticulture hardware/SaaS at the expense of biological and MRV categories
- **15 companies from the reference list dropped out**, including Naïo Technologies (robotics — hardware penalized), VitiBot (robotics), Semios (250+ employees — maturity score reduction), VineView, Tule Technologies, and Pellenc (1000+ employees — maturity score reduction)

**7.3 What the divergence signals**

The shift from the reference list toward biologicals and MRV is not arbitrary. It reflects:
1. Regenerative viticulture thesis specificity: precision viticulture hardware is generic agtech; biologicals and MRV are specific to the regenerative transition
2. Business model quality: biotech and SaaS models score higher on scalability and margin potential than hardware-dependent irrigation or robotics companies
3. Regulatory alignment: companies directly enabling pesticide reduction or carbon measurement benefit more from the EU regulatory wave than general vineyard management platforms

This divergence is a feature of the quantitative screening, not a defect. It should be treated as a hypothesis requiring validation, not a definitive replacement of analyst judgment.

## 9. Risks and Caveats

**9.1 Structural market risks**

**Fragmented customer base:** The wine industry is characterized by extreme fragmentation — thousands of small family estates alongside large cooperatives and corporate groups. AgTech adoption decisions are made estate-by-estate in most wine regions. Sales cycles are long, churn risk is high among small growers, and customer acquisition cost per hectare is often economically challenging for technology providers.

**Long agtech sales cycles:** Technology adoption in viticulture typically requires agronomist validation, trial-and-observe periods, and often full-season commitments before commercial contracts. This lengthens the time from first contact to revenue and requires substantial sales infrastructure relative to total addressable market.

**Hardware margin pressure:** Companies combining hardware with SaaS (Agrology, WiseConn, SupPlant) face persistent margin compression. Hardware gross margins of 40–60% at manufacturer level are compressed by deployment, installation, calibration, and maintenance costs. Software subscription layers improve the profile but require sustained hardware deployment at scale to justify investment.

**9.2 Technology-specific risks**

**Carbon market uncertainty:** Both Agrology and Biome Makers have commercial components linked to the agricultural carbon credit market. The voluntary carbon market experienced significant quality controversy in 2023–2024, with Verra (the largest carbon credit registry) facing credibility challenges that contracted institutional carbon credit demand. Regulatory carbon frameworks (Article 6 of the Paris Agreement, CBAM) provide a potential backstop but are not yet operational for agriculture. Companies whose revenue is primarily dependent on carbon credit premium pricing face real near-term demand risk.

**Registry acceptance gap:** No agricultural soil carbon sensor system has been formally accepted by a major voluntary carbon registry (Verra, Gold Standard, ACR) as a standalone approved MRV methodology as of the research date. This limits the most commercially compelling use case for Agrology and similar companies to sustainability reporting rather than audited credit generation.

**Database and IP defensibility:** Biome Makers' primary moat is its proprietary soil microbiome database. This claim is made but not independently verified. Investors must assess: (a) what proportion of the database is derived from unique proprietary samples vs. aggregated public reference databases (SILVA, NCBI); (b) what contractual protections exist against large channel partners internalizing comparable capabilities; and (c) how the database is protected from large agrichemical partners who could replicate it with sufficient investment.
