## Substituted Companies: Investability Assessment

### Sencrop — Original #3 (investability exclusion)

**Status: NO LONGER INDEPENDENTLY INVESTABLE — acquired January 2025 by ISAGRI**

**Findings:**
- Sencrop was acquired by Groupe ISAGRI (France's leading digital agriculture software group) effective January 7, 2025.
- ISAGRI is a private company. The acquisition absorbs Sencrop entirely; all prior Series B investors (Jerusalem Venture Partners, BPIfrance, Demeter IM, EIT Food, IRD Management, Stellar Impact, NCI Waterstart) have been exited.
- Sencrop's brand, weather station network, and app continue operating under ISAGRI's umbrella (ISAGRI's existing weather service, Météus, was absorbed into Sencrop).
- As of January 2025, Sencrop is a wholly-owned subsidiary of ISAGRI — not an independently investable company.
- **Conclusion:** Sencrop cannot be invested in separately. Investment would require acquiring ISAGRI or negotiating a carved-out structure, which is not available based on public information.

## Active Top-3 Companies: Red Flags and Data Gaps

### 1. Biome Makers

**Red flags:**

- **Revenue opacity:** Zero revenue figures have been disclosed publicly across all funding announcements, press releases, or impact reports. The 2024 Impact Report focuses on acres and farmers served, not financial KPIs. This is unusual for a Series B company and makes financial diligence reliant entirely on private data room access. The $20.6M "annual revenue" figure cited by some third-party aggregators (GrowJo, Craft.co) appears to be an algorithmic estimate — not a confirmed figure — and should not be relied upon.

- **Channel concentration risk:** A significant share of Biome Makers' commercial reach is mediated by a small number of key channel partners (Bayer, DISAGRO, UPL, Midwest Laboratories). If any of these partners internalize comparable capabilities (e.g., Bayer building its own soil microbiome diagnostics lab), Biome Makers' addressable market could contract materially. No long-term exclusivity terms have been disclosed.

- **Database defensibility question:** While Biome Makers claims "the world's largest soil microbiome database" (55M+ taxonomic references), it is not independently verified whether this claim is based on unique sequences, redundant samples, or aggregated public databases (e.g., SILVA, NCBI). Investors should request a clear explanation of database composition and proprietary vs. licensed content.

- **Competitor Miraterra:** The acquisition of Trace Genomics by Miraterra (2024) re-activates a competitor that was previously losing momentum. Miraterra now integrates soil, plant, and food measurement — a broader platform than BeCrop. The competitive implications of a potentially re-capitalized Miraterra are unclear.

**Data gaps (not publicly verifiable):**
- Total funding prior to Series B (pre-2024 rounds)
- Current ARR, MRR, or revenue trajectory
- Customer retention and repeat purchase rates
- Unit economics (cost-per-test vs. selling price)
- Headcount breakdown (commercial vs. R&D vs. lab operations)
- Patent portfolio details

**Investability concerns:** None. Biome Makers is a standard equity-investable private company with institutional VC backing. Investment access is the only question.

### 2. Biorizon Biotech

**Red flags:**

- **PE ownership structure complexity:** Moira Capital Partners holds 45.5% of Biorizon Biotech (acquired December 2021). The remaining ~54.5% is held by management and founding partners. This minority PE stake creates governance complexity: investors seeking a new position would need either to (a) negotiate a secondary purchase from Moira Capital or founders, or (b) participate in a new funding round — neither of which is currently publicly announced. Moira Capital's expected exit window (5–7 years from December 2021) implies an exit process beginning approximately 2026–2028, creating near-term overhang uncertainty.

- **Financial opacity — extreme:** No revenue figures, growth rates, or financial metrics have been disclosed in any public source beyond qualitative statements ("doubled sales in 2022"). The €23M capital deployment plan (announced at Moira Capital entry in 2022) has no public progress update. Investors cannot assess current financial health from public information.

- **Viticulture evidence gap:** Despite scoring highly on viticulture segment relevance, Biorizon provides no named vineyard customers, no published viticulture field trial data with quantified outcomes, and no wine-industry-specific case studies in public sources. All vineyard relevance is inferred from product descriptions ("suitable for viticulture"). This is a meaningful evidentiary gap for a regenerative viticulture thesis investor.

- **Biologicals market competition:** The European biostimulants market is attracting significant investment from Syngenta (Valagro acquisition), Novonesis (chr. Hansen + Novozymes merger), and BASF (biologicals expansion). Biorizon's ability to maintain its market position against companies with orders-of-magnitude greater resources is not demonstrated in public data.

- **Production risk:** Large-scale photobioreactor operations (outdoor/indoor raceway and tubular systems) are known to face contamination, yield variability, and scale-up challenges. No public information addresses Biorizon's production consistency or yield metrics at current scale.

**Data gaps (not publicly verifiable):**
- Revenue (any period)
- Gross margin profile
- Vineyard-specific trial data with measurable outcomes
- Patent holdings on proprietary strains or extraction processes
- Status of €23M investment plan
- Terms of Moira Capital's shareholder agreement (exit rights, tag-along)
- EU biopesticide registration pipeline and timelines

**Investability concerns:** SIGNIFICANT. The PE minority ownership structure makes direct equity investment by a new investor difficult without a secondary market transaction or new round. Combined with extreme financial opacity, this company is currently most accessible as a commercial/partnership relationship rather than a portfolio investment.

### 3. Agrology

**Red flags:**

- **Team size is existential risk:** 12 employees operating a hardware deployment company with named clients including Duckhorn Portfolio, Jordan Vineyards, and Joseph Phelps is impressive — but also precarious. A single key employee departure (especially a founder) could impair operations and customer relationships. No succession or key-person risk mitigation measures are evident from public information.

- **Total funding is very low for hardware company:** Estimated $6–9M total equity funding (undisclosed seed round + NSF grants) is very low for a company manufacturing proprietary sensor hardware and deploying it across multiple high-maintenance sites. Hardware companies typically require $10–20M+ at Series A. The USDA grant ($5M) is non-dilutive but shared across a consortium and restricted to project activities — it does not provide unrestricted operating capital.

- **Carbon market demand uncertainty:** Agrology's core commercial proposition depends on vineyard operators willing to pay for MRV tools that can generate or verify carbon credits. If voluntary carbon markets remain depressed (as they have been in 2023–2025 due to quality controversies), the premium for sensor-validated vs. model-based MRV may not materialize at the scale needed for commercial viability.

- **No carbon registry validation:** As of the time of research, Agrology's Arbiter sensor data has not been accepted by any carbon registry (Verra, Gold Standard, ACR) as a formally approved MRV methodology. Until registry acceptance is achieved, the sensors function primarily as research and sustainability-reporting tools — not as generators of audited carbon credits. This limits the most compelling commercial use case.

- **Geographic concentration:** 100% of named customer deployments are in California wine country. Agrology has no international presence and limited presence outside California in the US. Any California-specific risk (wildfire disruption, drought policy changes, economic slowdown in luxury wine segment) concentrates risk in a single geography.

**Data gaps (not publicly verifiable):**
- Total seed funding amount (undisclosed)
- ARR / revenue / customer contract values
- Hardware manufacturing approach (proprietary vs. contract)
- Carbon registry application status
- N2O sensor commercial pricing and customer adoption (sensor announced; commercial availability stated for 2025 — no confirmation of commercial contracts found)
- Founder background details (full bios not publicly available)
- IP protection details (patents not publicly listed)

**Investability concerns:** LOW for general investability (company is standard equity-structured); HIGH for risk-adjusted return given stage, team size, and hardware complexity. Best suited for impact-first investors with long time horizons and appetite for seed-stage hardware risk.

## Cross-Cutting Risk Signals

1. **Financial opacity across all three companies:** None of the three companies discloses revenue or financial performance publicly. All investor analysis in this pipeline is based on qualitative signals, funding round data, and inferred metrics. Full financial diligence requires private data room access.

2. **Voluntary carbon market headwinds:** Both Biome Makers (soil health → carbon) and Agrology (carbon MRV) have components of their thesis dependent on carbon credit markets and ESG reporting mandates. The 2023–2025 carbon market quality crisis (Verra voluntary credits controversy) has created commercial uncertainty. Regulatory tailwinds (EU CSRD, SEC climate disclosure) partially offset this, but the timeline is uncertain.

3. **Biologicals market heating:** Both Biome Makers and Biorizon operate in the soil health/biologicals space, which is attracting growing competition from large agrichemical companies pivoting to bio. The "natural inputs" segment is becoming contested terrain, and pricing power for smaller specialists may erode over time.
